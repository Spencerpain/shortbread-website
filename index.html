Our Story

Four Decades of Shortbread Perfection

What began in 1986 as a passion project in a Toronto coach house has grown into Canada's most celebrated artisan shortbread. Every batch is still made by hand, in small quantities, using time-honoured Scottish recipes elevated with unexpected flavour combinations.

We believe shortbread deserves to be more than a holiday treat. Our sweet and savoury range proves that these buttery, crumbly biscuits can be a year-round indulgence — at the dinner table, at cocktail hour, or straight from the tin.

History & Heritage

Did You Know…

Shortbread has a rich and fascinating history stretching back centuries. Here's the story behind the biscuit.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿
Scottish Origins

Shortbread originated in Scotland. Scotland's cattle-farming tradition naturally led to dairy-heavy cuisine, with butter and cheese flavouring many traditional foods.

🍞
From Bannock to Biscuit

Shortbread evolved from medieval "biscuit bread" — leftover bread dough dried in low ovens until hardened. "Biscuit" derives from the French for "twice cooked." Yeast gradually gave way to butter, transforming it into shortbread.

🛖
Made to Last

"Bread would go mouldy on long treks or out with the cattle for months. Shortbread would last — and went very well with stew." Its durability made it an essential companion for Scottish herders.

👑
Mary, Queen of Scots

Mary, Queen of Scots favoured "Petticoat Tails" — thin, crisp, buttery shortbread flavoured with caraway seeds. The name possibly derives from the French "petites gatelles" (little cakes) or from the distinctive thin wedge shape.

🎊
Hogmanay & New Year

Scottish Hogmanay celebrations featured shortbread flavoured with almond or candied citrus peel. "The custom of eating shortbread at New Year has its origins in the ancient pagan Yule Cakes which symbolized the sun." Scotland's "first footer" tradition involves offering shortbread to the first guest of the New Year.

💍
Scottish Wedding Tradition

Shortbread is traditional at Scottish weddings. Historical tradition involved the groom's mother breaking a shortbread over the bride's head — soft loaves indicated approval, hard ones suggested disapproval!

🔵
Three Classic Shapes

Traditional shortbread takes three forms: one large circle divided into segments ("Petticoat Tails"), individual round biscuits ("Shortbread Rounds"), or a thick rectangular slab cut into "fingers."

🌍
A Global Tradition

While Scottish in origin, similar butter-based confections appear worldwide — all sharing the basic ingredients, distinctive texture, and appealing flavour.

🇨🇦 Quebec — Sablés & Biscuits d'Érable 🇮🇹 Italy — Frollini 🇪🇸 Spain — Polvorones 🇫🇷 France — Palets Bretons 🇨🇳 China — Mooncakes 🇨🇱 Chile — Alfajor 🇮🇳 India — Naan Khatai
